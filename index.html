Battle of Stalingrad

The Battle of Stalingrad was a major battle fought during World War II (WWII) between Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union. The battle took place from August 23, 1942, to February 2, 1943, and was fought in and around the city of Stalingrad (now known as Volgograd) in southern Russia.

The battle was one of the bloodiest in human history, with an estimated 2 million casualties. The German forces, led by General Friedrich Paulus, were ultimately defeated by the Soviet forces, led by General Georgy Zhukov.

The Battle of Stalingrad is considered a turning point in WWII, as it marked the beginning of the end for Nazi Germany. The defeat at Stalingrad was a major blow to the morale of the German troops and marked the first time that they had been forced to retreat on the Eastern Front.

The battle was also significant because it was fought entirely in an urban setting, with both sides fighting for control of every building, street, and alleyway. The fighting was brutal and often hand-to-hand, with both sides sustaining heavy losses.

In the end, the Soviet Union emerged victorious, and the German forces were forced to surrender. The Battle of Stalingrad remains one of the most important and well-known battles of WWII, and is remembered as a testament to the resilience and bravery of the Soviet people.
